Technical & Legislative Provisions

An objective, clause-by-clause analysis of the amendments proposed in Kenya Gazette Supplement No. 113 (National Assembly Bills No. 26). Here is how these provisions work and their mechanical impact.

A The Technology & Digital Payments Sector

1. Treatment of Merchant & Interchange Fees (Section 2 Amendment)

The Clause: The bill expands the definition of "management or professional fee" to explicitly include "interchange fees and merchant service fees arising from transactions that use a card as a means of payment".

Objective Impact: Merchant service fees and interchange feesâ€”which banks, card networks (like Visa and Mastercard), and digital payment processors charge merchants to process paymentsâ€”are now officially categorized as professional services. This makes them subject to Withholding Tax (WHT). While this expands the tax base into digital payment infrastructure, financial technology (Fintech) firms and commercial banks argue it increases their tax compliance overhead and administrative reporting duties.

2. The Software & Cloud Platform "Royalty" Overhaul

The Clause: The definition of "royalty" has been entirely replaced. It now explicitly covers:

  • Any software (whether proprietary or off-the-shelf), licensing, development, training, maintenance, or support fees.
  • Access, usage, or participation rights in proprietary digital platforms, payment networks, card schemes, processing systems, clearing, switching, or settlement systems.
  • Software distribution models where regular payments are routed through a distributor.

Objective Impact: Historically, software maintenance, training, and off-the-shelf software purchases were often treated as business profits (which are generally not subject to withholding tax). By reclassifying these transactions as royalties, the bill subjects all software licenses, cloud SaaS subscriptions, IT support agreements, and card settlement system participation fees to Withholding Tax. This captures revenues generated by global and domestic software vendors, though local startups and corporate IT departments will face increased withholding tax compliance obligations.

B Employment, Corporate & Betting Rules

1. Gratuity & Long-Term Contracts (Section 3 Amendment)

The Clause: Contributions to employment gratuity are deductible/tax-exempt provided: (i) the contract is for a continuous period of at least 3 years, (ii) total contributions do not exceed 31% of the employee's basic salary, and (iii) the employee is not receiving deductions under separate pension schemes (Section 22A).

Objective Impact: This provision standardizes long-term contract benefits across industries. Proponents suggest it protects contract workers by offering clear tax relief for gratuity savings, while corporate departments must audit contract structures to ensure compliance with the 3-year and 31% thresholds to qualify for the tax deduction.

2. Betting, Withdrawals & Winnings (Section 2 & 10 Amendments)

The Clause: Integrates provisions with the new Gambling Control Act, 2025. Defines "withdrawals" as payouts paid to players, and "winnings" as payouts from lotteries/competitions (excluding the stake). Winnings are officially added to Section 10, making them subject to Withholding Tax.

Objective Impact: This tightens the regulatory and tax framework for the online gaming and betting platforms. While it secures additional revenues for public initiatives, betting tech platforms will need to integrate automatic tax deduction mechanisms directly into their checkout/withdrawal gateways.

3. Sale of Scrap Metal (Section 7 Amendment)

The Clause: Adds the "sale of scrap metal" to Section 10 of the Income Tax Act, subjecting transactions to Withholding Tax.

Objective Impact: This aims to bring the highly lucrative and historically informal scrap metal trade into the structured tax net. Players in the recycling and scrap metal manufacturing supply chains will be required to withhold and remit taxes on these transactions.

C Specialized Taxes & Official Timelines

1. Non-Resident Rental Income Tax (New Section 6B)

The Clause: Introduces a final tax on rental income derived by non-residents from properties in Kenya. It establishes a simplified digital registration framework. Returns must be filed and tax paid by the 20th day of the following month, unless a resident agent collects the rent and deducts the withholding tax instead.

Objective Impact: This creates a structured, digital-first method to collect taxes from foreign real estate investors. The simplified registration portal minimizes red tape for foreign owners, though failure to register or file returns could trigger standard non-compliance penalties.

2. Maritime Shipping Tax Payments (Section 6 Amendment)

The Clause: Tax charged on maritime shipping (Section 9) must be paid within five days after the payment is received or the ship leaves the port, whichever is earlier.

Objective Impact: This significantly speeds up the tax remittance timeline for maritime shipping operations, shifting from historical filing cycles to immediate payment, which improves the Treasury's short-term liquidity.

3. Housing Loan Interest Deductions (Section 10 Amendment)

The Clause: Employees who construct, purchase, or improve a house using loans advanced by the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) can deduct loan interest up to Ksh 360,000 from their taxable income.

Objective Impact: This provides a specific fiscal benefit designed to lower housing finance costs for eligible public sector employees, encouraging long-term homeownership.

Simplified Guide

Finance Bill 2026 Explained Simply

A plain-language breakdown of what changed, what's new, and what it means for you.

Overview

What Changed Between 2025 and 2026?

Finance Bill 2025 introduced major tax reforms in Kenya. Finance Bill 2026 focused on enforcing those reforms more aggressively.

Finance Bill 2025
  • Introduced reforms
  • Expanded taxes
  • Added new tax systems
  • Focused on revenue collection
Finance Bill 2026
  • Strengthened enforcement
  • Improved digital monitoring
  • Automated compliance systems
  • Focused on enforcement and visibility
Simple Meaning: 2025 opened the door to more taxes and compliance. 2026 tightened control and increased monitoring.
New Additions

What Was Newly Added in Finance Bill 2026?

1. 60% Deemed Dividend Rule

Closely held companies that retain profits for too long may now be taxed as if they distributed 60% of those profits as dividends.

2. Tax Amnesty Program

Businesses and individuals can clear old taxes and receive waiver of penalties and interest if principal taxes are paid.

3. Crypto Monitoring

Virtual Asset Service Providers must now report crypto transactions, identities, wallet addresses, and values to KRA.

4. VAT Clawback Rules

Businesses may be forced to refund VAT previously claimed if products later become VAT exempt.

5. Digital Card Network Taxes

Visa, Mastercard, and payment processing systems are now included in withholding tax systems.

6. Phone Activation Excise Duty

Phones are now taxed during activation on Kenyan networks instead of only at importation.

Reduced

What Was Reduced?

1. Digital Asset Tax

The Digital Asset Tax reduced from 3% to 1.5%.

2. Financial Sector Restrictions

Leasing and lending institutions received clarification and exemptions from restrictive EBITDA interest caps.

Good News: Crypto traders and some financial institutions experienced lighter tax pressure.
Stricter Rules

What Became Stricter?

Automated KRA Assessments

KRA can now estimate taxes using:

  • eTIMS data
  • Bank records
  • Payroll systems
  • Invoices
  • Digital transactions

Higher Penalties

Failure to issue eTIMS invoices now attracts:

  • KSh 10,000 minimum fine for individuals
  • KSh 100,000 minimum fine for companies

Shorter Objection Timelines

Appeals now use calendar days instead of working days.

VAT Claim Window Reduced

VAT claims reduced from 24 months to 12 months.

Legislative Alignment

Tax Legislation & Finance Bill Alignment Update

Technical review of VAT, Excise Duty, Income Tax, and regulatory amendments.

VAT Reforms

VAT Structural Reclassification

The Finance Bill introduces significant VAT reforms by moving selected products from the Zero-Rated category to the Exempt category.

Important Distinction:
Zero-Rated Supplies: Businesses can recover input VAT.
Exempt Supplies: Businesses cannot recover input VAT, increasing operational costs and potentially raising consumer prices.

New Additions to Exempt Supplies (Amendment No. 31)

  • Mobile telephone handsets
  • Electric motorcycles
  • Electric passenger buses
  • Transportation of sugarcane from farms to milling factories
  • Solar products and green energy equipment

Clarification on Bread VAT Status

Ordinary bread remains Zero-Rated under Item 13A of the Second Schedule of the VAT Act. Bakers can still recover input VAT, and the retail cost of ordinary bread is protected from additional VAT burden.

Excise Duty

Excise Duty Adjustments

The Finance Bill introduces a substantial increase in Excise Duty on imported mobile phones.

Category Previous Rate Amended Rate
Imported Cellular Phones 10% 25%
Impact: The 15% increase is expected to increase government customs revenue, encourage local phone assembly and manufacturing, and raise retail prices for imported mobile devices.
Regulatory Changes

Regulatory and Procedural Amendments

Tax Procedures Act (TPA) Overhaul

The Tax Procedures Act has been amended to strengthen digital tax administration and improve compliance monitoring.

  • Enhanced real-time transaction reporting.
  • Improved automated accounting integration.
  • Stronger KRA oversight mechanisms.
  • Adjusted tax dispute resolution timelines.

Road Maintenance Levy Reduction

The Finance Bill also proposes a reduction in the Road Maintenance Levy.

Expected Benefits:
  • Lower fuel pump prices.
  • Reduced transport and logistics costs.
  • Relief for businesses dependent on distribution networks.
